Project 1: Personas

A link to this project page: https://cunningcapybara20.github.io/Personas/

Context/Problem

This project was meant to allow us to understand different ways that products and UX designers achieve the goal of a learnable and coherent design. For this project I looked at a rice cooker as my interface and analyzed the failures and successes of its design as a usable and learnable product. In order to fully understand how we should go about designing apps and webpages later in the course, this project was meant to challenge our thinking and further explore what good design is.

Research/Feedback

This work is done very often with all products, physical and digital in order to produce the most learnable and usable product in each individual case. The audience for this work is inevitably the user of the product. Because we, as designers, want to have the product not only be functional, but also make sense to users and not confuse them. This means that we have to use test subjects, random people in this instance, to explain the shortcomings and benefits of similar products to iterate on their strengths or weaknesses.

Design Iterations

This project in particular did not have many design iterations, since it was mostly a research piece, but this submission page was prior to me really knowing anything about css and so the page itself is very poorly designed. Lots of white space and it is not very appealing.

Conclusion

This project was very insightful into the right frame of mind for the rest of the course. The focus of a UI/UX designer is very different than that of someone strictly trying to make a functional product, so it is a interesting twist on things that I have done previously. Furthermore, it was very helpful looking at a real world example rather than a webpage for this because it emphasized the issues with poor design and the benefits of good design.

Project 2: Responsive Redesign

A link to this project page: https://cunningcapybara20.github.io/Redesigned-Site/

Context/Problem

The responsive redesign project was our first jump into actually designing a webpage for ourselves. We were trying to solve the problem of a webpage that we knew had bad design and giving it better design. We care to do this because a lot of websites now have terrible design and need to be updated, so as a part of this project we took a shot at it and tested our skills.

Research/Feedback

Redesigning websites is not a foreign concept, but clearly the ones that we chose were in dire need of some work. The audience for this project is the owners and users of the webpage we redesigned as they would hopefully be able to employ those changes to have a better, more functional website for their organization/brand/restaurant. The redesign was meant to take the lessons we learned in the previous project and deploy it into something new with a redesigned website page in this project.

Design Iterations

I chose to look at the home page for the cookie shop "Feed the Cheeks". For my project I decided to keep a lot of the same themes as the original website, like color and logos. But, I felt that the home page didn't have enough to draw the user into interacting and had the home page as a shell that didn't offer as much as it could. So, I decided to have more pictures on said page in my design and I felt that they didn't do enough to market themselves on the home page with potential promotions or changes to the store (they are coming out with a new store on campus), so I made more space to be able to advertise these important aspects of the shop without sacrificing the identity of the store and website.

Conclusion

Similar to the Personas project, this project gave great insight into what great design is and the tradeoffs to what comes with that design. The balance between what you need on the page and what you dont. Between too much white space and too little. Between colors that are too bright and saturated or too dull and faded. There is a lot of freedom in this project and it ends up making each decision difficult. They all come into play and each one can offset previous choices. This project showed the importance of the coherent plan when designing.

Project 3: A/B Testing & Eye Tracking

A link to this project page: https://cunningcapybara20.github.io/Redesigned-Site/

Context/Problem

This project was meant to teach us one way to design our own webpage and it helped solve the issue during this testing of how efficient our design is. During this project we had two versions of the webpage (a cart for a restaurant's online ordering), and we had subjects perform a few tasks to see if they could complete it quickly and effectively. We care about this because if we have a design that is too cluttering and visually distracting it takes away from the user's ability to perform the task.

Research/Feedback

This work is done often with design as using two or three or many different prototypes to see which is most effective is a good way to test multiple designs. Our audience is eventually our end users, but the data is very useful for us as the designers to create the most effective product. We want our users to think and act honestly during this testing because the point of their work is to give insight into how good the design is through user trials. Their thoughts on the different designs is thorugh their actions If they are unable to perform the task effectively and quickly, then the design must not be ideal.

Design Iterations

In our designs we had two different versions of the cart. The first showed the items all on the same screen and had pictures of them. The second ordered them without pictures. This was done to test the necessity for more or less visual clutter. We found that the former was the better option as the best option as the visuals helped users find their choice from the menu the easiest.

Conclusion

This gave my group and I valuable insight into how the different tradeoffs to design can affect user interactions. For example, ordering off a menu of food you want to see pictures in order to have a good sense of what you are getting and you as the customer feel more confident in your choice. This is good to know in the case of a online menu, but that same principle might now apply to some other online page. This is very useful information for us as young designers.

Project 4: Iterative Design

A link to this project page: https://pensivepossum090.github.io/Iterative-Design/

Context/Problem

This project was meant to show us that the first prototype or design we come up with may not be perfect. We need to iterate and test upon our ideas in order to come to the best result. This is a familiar concept with many things, but in order to have the best outcome, it may take more than one attempt. This is an important idea in design and UI/UX because our idea could be very strong but we are not implementing it in a way that is ideal to what our final product could/should look and interact like.

Research/Feedback

Iterating on designs is a very common concept in any design field, so here it is not surprise. This projects audience is ourselves in order to create the best product for our users. We would like to believe that everything we design is perfect from the gates, but realistically it may not be and so it is important to iterate. We want to be continuously thinking about this with design so that we have the best possible product and continue to change and improve it over the time that we work on it.

Design Iterations

In this project, we made an app for the company "NFTScoring". Who we designed an NFT marketplace app for that would house all of the relevant features. We originally wanted to have there be different pages for buying and selling that were very similar, but then through our feedback and iterations decided that things were too similar and needed to be differentiated more in order to decrease the possibilities of users mistaking the pages for each other and performing tasks they didn't mean to.

Conclusion

This project gave great insight into what it actually means to fully design a digital app/webpage. We as designers were able to look at the whole process and understand exactly what goes into the iterations and understanding how the users interact with what we originally designed.
